Question: Do you think it’s fair for the LDS church not to allow the father of the bride to witness her temple marriage because he can’t qualify for a temple recommend?

It is amazing to me how many questions I see where the answer reflects a conversation I’ve recently had with my 6 year old. When he doesn’t get his way or is told “no,” he will often bemoan how unfair our decision is. Because he doesn’t know what “fair” means. What he is actually saying is he doesn’t like that he isn’t getting his way, because he wants to do what he wants with no consequences.

What does “fair” mean:

impartial and just, without favoritism or discrimination

The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints has extremely clear standards for what it takes to go to the temple. One must be recommended as someone worthy to enter by both their bishopric and stake presidency. To determine worthiness, the individual sits down for an interview with a member of each group and is asked a set of standard questions. Receiving a recommend is entirely based on if you are actually living according to those questions. For example: “do you obey the Law of Chastity” or “are you honest in your dealings with your fellow man?”

Every single person who has a temple recommend has one because they are living according to the same impartial standard. No one is discriminated against for any reason. If you are living in accordance with the standards set by the temple recommend interview questions, you may enter the temple. If you are not, you cannot. This process is by definition, fair.

Further: weddings, especially temple weddings, aren’t sudden. At least, not sudden enough that this would normally be something sprung on a parent last minute. If you are a member of the Church, you know what the standard is to enter the temple. If you are not a member (but your child getting married is, because that is how they can enter the temple), then I do apologize that your child decided not to tell you this until the last minute apparently.

Feelings aside, the one thing this process is not is unfair. It would be highly discriminatory and showing favoritism to allow this one individual in who doesn’t qualify and to not let in the other billions of individuals who also don’t currently qualify.

I quite truly am sorry you find yourself in this situation. But exactly like I tell my six year old: just because you don’t like it, doesn’t mean it isn’t fair.

Once you decide to buy a plot, make sure you have all documents in place. Without these, your purchase will be delayed. Having all the proper legal documentation will help protect your land and home from any disputes in the future. Consult a lawyer to help you with every step of the documentation process.

Most of the required documents can be grouped into two types - legal and personal.

Legal documents: These documents are essential, and missing even one of these can result in a delay in purchase.

They include: -

Mother Deed: This is taken from the seller. The Mother Deed is the main document to determine ownership of the property. It traces the land’s chain of ownership and provides information about the history of the plot.

Sales Deed: The Sales Deed records the transfer of land ownership from the seller to the buyer. You can get it validated at the sub-registrar's office.

Power of Attorney(POA): If the seller of the land is not the owner, they should have a Power of Attorney that authorizes them to sell the plot. Always check the Power of Attorney when buying from any seller.

Encumbrance Certificate: An Encumbrance Certificate documents all the transactions related to the land. It serves as proof that the land you are buying is free of any monetary or legal bindings. It is obtained from the sub-registrar’s office where land is registered.

Katha certificate: A Khata Certificate is necessary to obtain a building license. It contains property details like location, size, built-up area etc. and is necessary for the payment of property tax and to obtain a building license. It is obtained from the Assistant Revenue Officer.

Land Clearance: If you wish to convert agricultural land to non-agricultural land this certificate becomes very essential.

Record Of Rights (ROR) certificate: It is obtained from the Tehsildar’s office

Personal documents: Personal documents are purely for verification purposes: Aadhar, Voter ID card and PAN card.

Things to keep in mind:

In case the seller is not the owner, check the ‘power of attorney’ document.

To make sure measurements mentioned by seller are accurate, get a survey sketch of the land from Survey Department.

If there are more than one owner, make sure to get ‘release certificate’ from all owners.
