The answer is in the same verse (emphasis mine):

And I, Nephi, did build a temple; and I did construct it after the manner of the temple of Solomon save it were not built of so many precious things; for they were not to be found upon the land, wherefore, it could not be built like unto Solomon’s temple. But the manner of the construction was like unto the temple of Solomon; and the workmanship thereof was exceedingly fine.

So Nephi is saying he built a temple like Solomon’s temple, but not from the same materials and likely not the same size. It would also follow that it didn’t require the same number of workers or take the same amount of time to build.

Sometime after his father Lehi’s death, Nephi talks about a separation between older brothers Laman and Lemuel, Ishmael’s sons, and their respective wives and children; while the remainder of the family followed Nephi. He also mentions 30 and then 40 years having elapsed since their departure from Jerusalem. The next major time milestone was passed when Nephi handed his records down to his younger brother Jacob 55 years after leaving Jerusalem, dying soon afterward. This gives them at least 25 years during which a temple could have been built, and possibly more time than that. (The assumption that Nephi reported every event in chronological order is an assumption, and far too many criticisms of the Book of Mormon and other scriptures are based on bad assumptions.)

If all of that is too abstract, let’s rephrase the verse a bit with another example:

And I did build a tower; and I did construct it after the manner of the tower of Eiffel save it were not built of so many precious things; for they were not to be found upon the land, wherefore, it could not be built like unto Eiffel’s tower. But the manner of the construction was like unto the tower of Eiffel; and the workmanship thereof was exceedingly fine.

We know the important things about the Eiffel Tower, such as its dimensions, how much iron was used, and how long it took to build it. No exact replica of it has been built anywhere else in the world, though there have been smaller structures that were obviously influenced by it.

This tower is obviously not made of the same materials as the original Eiffel Tower. It’s obviously not the same size, nor did it take the same time or workforce to build it. But it’s easy to recognize what it was modeled after.

All that verse indicates is that its manner of construction (probably the overall appearance and layout) was similar in design to the temple of Solomon. Not that it was equal in most ways.

It doesn't specify the material used for construction, but given the limited labor as you noted, it might have been a wooden structure, at least from the start. Sections can later be replaced with stone over time as needed, and not necessarily even in Nephi's lifetime.

Also, we don't know how long Nephi's temple took to build. Could have taken seven years, five, ten, fifteen, thirty…the timeframe isn't given.

Another thing that isn't known is the size of Nephi's temple or how much material was needed.

So the information just isn't enough to give any informed guess about how much labor was required or how long it took, and thus you have no basis to say that the construction was utterly impossible. You can't deduce impossibility from the practical restraints when you don't even know what those restraints were.

There were 20 or more people in the Lehi party which crossed the sea (1 Nephi 4:35; 7:6; 16:7; 18:7; 2 Nephi 5:6). The nine or more families which departed in 592 B.C. (Lehi, Ishmael, Leman, Lemuel, Nephi, Zoram, Sam, Ishmael's 1st son, Ishmael's 2nd son) and multiplied (2 Nephi 5:13) could have been 70 or more 22 years later (2 Nephi 5:28). It is not unreasonable to think that 35 to 45 of these might have helped build a temple (2 Nephi 5:6).

2 Nephi 5:16 says that the "manner of construction [of their temple] was like unto the temple of Solomon" but the materials according to the context were different (not so precious). Solomon's temple was not much larger than many of our meetinghouses measuring 90 feet in length by 30 feet in width and height (Book of Mormon Student Manual, p. 64). The time it took to build the temple is not actually specified, but a similar temple was finished in less than three years when the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints was about five years old (the Kirtland temple was started in June 1833 and finished in March 1836). The Nauvoo temple was constructed in five years (1841-1846) during a period of intense persecution of the Church. That a smaller group could have constructed a temple in less than 30 years (2 Nephi 5:28) seems quite practical to me.

It’s also likely that those describing the work of building Solomon’s temple exaggerated the numbers to give them symbolic significance. Numbers were often rounded up to numbers with spiritual significance. Seven is one of those numbers. I can’t even imagine 150,000 men working seven years on a 90-foot by 30-foot building. I can’t even imagine 150 men working that long on a building that size much less 150,000.

Biblical numbers are primarily based on the decimal system, which is of Hamito-Egyptian origin. The sexagesimal system, however, which ultimately derives from Sumerian usage, also plays an important role in Scripture, and since 60 is divisible by ten and five, the two methods of reckoning easily coalesce. The numbers in the Bible range from one (Gen. 1:5) to 100,000,000 (Dan. 7:10), though the latter figure is to be regarded as a hyperbole rather than a literal numerical expression. The largest number to be understood literally is that given in I Chronicles 21:5 in connection with David’s census: 1,100,000 men from Israel plus 470,000 from Judah that drew the sword (but cf. the smaller figures in II Sam. 24:9). The idea of infinity in the mathematical sense (in contrast to the theological concept of God’s unlimited powers) is not found in the Bible. However, it is recognized that there are limits to the human ability to count (Gen. 13:16; 41:49).

Symbolic and Rhetorical Use

Biblical numbers are not always intended to be taken at their face value. They are often used indefinitely – as round figures – or rhetorically, for emphasis or in a hyperbolic sense…. Many numbers are noteworthy for their symbolic nuances.… Especially significant is the biblical use of sacred numbers, which play an important religious role. There is, in addition, a distinct tendency in Scripture to achieve numerical harmony or symmetry. This aspect has been worked out in considerable detail for Genesis, notably its early chapters, by U. SEVEN. Seven played an exceptionally important role in antiquity. It was sacred to Semitic and other peoples, including the Egyptians, Assyrians, Persians, and the Vedic folk in India. Its importance is often derived from the worship of the seven heavenly bodies: the sun, moon, and the five planets. It is also pointed out that the seven-day week was approximately a quarter of the lunar month (29. days), and that the Pleiades (Amos 5:8) were thought to comprise seven stars. Others see the origin of the number’s prominence in the fact that it is composed of the sacred numbers three and four, or in the “unrelated” character of seven in the series one to ten. Like the Sumerians, the biblical writers often add seven to a large number to indicate a very big figure. U. Cassuto writes: “It clearly follows that the chronology of the Book of Genesis as a whole is also founded on the dual principle of the sexagesimal system and the addition of seven” (From Adam to Noah, in bibl., 259). In the Bible the number seven is connected with every aspect of religious life in every period: e.g., the clean beasts in the ark (Gen. 7:2ff.); Abraham’s covenant with Abimelech (Gen. 21:28–30); cleansing from leprosy (Lev. 14); the festivals (Lev. 23; Deut. 16:9); Balaam’s altars (Num. 23); the induction of the priests and the consecration of the altars (Ex. 29:35–37); sacrifices (Gen. 8:20; Num. 28:11; Job 42:8; I Chron. 15:26); the Temple furnishings (I Kings 7:17); the menorah (Ex. 25:31–37; Zech. 4:2); the Temple steps (Ezek. 40:22); the width of the Temple entrance (Ezek. 41:3); the sprinkling of blood (Lev. 4:6, 17;16:14; Num. 19:4) and the like. The innate, mystic power of seven is exemplified in Joshua 6:4, 8, 13 (Jericho); Judges 16:13, 19 (Samson); and II Kings 5:10 (Naaman). It also occurs in connection with punishment (Gen. 4:24; Lev. 26:18; Deut. 28:7, 25; II Sam. 21:6; Prov. 6:31; Dan. 4:13, 20, 29; 9:27). In relation to time, seven represents a fitting (or sacred) period (Gen. 1:3ff.; 8:12; 50:10; Ex. 7:25; Lev. 8:33; Josh. 6). More generally it indicates a complete or round number of moderate size (Isa. 4:1; 11:15; Micah 5:4; Ps. 12:7 [6]; Prov. 26:16, 25; Job 1:2; Esth. 1:10; 2:9). In Deuteronomy 7:1 it is equated with “many.”…

A THOUSAND. A thousand and its multiples are frequently used in the Bible as round numbers indicating a large amount. Etymologically the Hebrew word elef (“thousand”) denotes “a crowd,” and hence at times has the sense of “tribe,” “clan,” or designates a military unit, which does not necessarily comprise 1,000 (Ex. 18:21; Deut. 33:17; Judg. 6:15). Flinders Petrie (Researches in Sinai), interpreting elef to mean a family or tent, reduced the figure for the first census to 5,500, and to 5,730 for the second. Multiples of 1,000 are often hyperbolic expressions (Lev. 26:8; Deut. 32:30; I Sam. 18:7; Ps. 3:7[6]; Song 5:10). Seventy thousand (II Sam. 24:15) and 1,000,000 (Dan. 7:10; I Chron. 21:5; 22:14; II Chron. 14:8) are globular figures indicative of a vast number, while “thousands of ten thousands” (Gen. 24:60) and “ten thousand times ten thousand” (Dan. 7:10) are imaginative numerical ultimates. Similarly high figures are found in Ugaritic literature.

Let’s begin with some context. 2 Nephi 5 starts off with Nephi crying to the Lord because of the anger of his older brothers, who resented Nephi being a ruler over the people. Verses 5–6 say: “And it came to pass that the Lord did warn me, that I, Nephi, should depart from them and flee into the wilderness, and all those who would go with me. Wherefore, it came to pass that I, Nephi, did take my family, and also Zoram and his family, and Sam, mine elder brother and his family, and Jacob and Joseph, my younger brethren, and also my sisters, and all those who would go with me.” Verse 7 continues: And we did take our tents and whatsoever things were possible for us, and did journey in the wilderness for the space of many days. And after we had journeyed for the space of many days we did pitch our tents.” Note that this does not seem to be any particularly large number of people. It’s mostly the descendants of Lehi. Maybe 100 people at most. The area they are in is described as “wilderness.” That implies that there were no other people living there. Just where this would be is open to considerable question. Guatemala? Peru? Illinois? Upstate New York? I have heard all of these proposed by various LDS.

2 Nephi 5:11 says: “And the Lord was with us; and we did prosper exceedingly; for we did sow seed, and we did reap again in abundance. And we began to raise flocks, and herds, and animals of every kind.” This reminds us of the Book of Mormon’s anachronism difficulty. “Flocks” here might refer to ducks or turkeys, which later Native Americans are known to have had. But what animal does “herds” refer to? Pre-Columbian Native Americans probably didn’t have domesticated sheep, cows, pigs, goats, or horses. The closest herd animal might be the llama, but those were known only in certain parts of South America. As to the seeds, the BoM itself indicates that these seeds were carried in their ship by Lehi and his family (1 Nephi 18:6, 22–25). However, pre-Columbian Native Americans didn’t have any of the domesticated plants known in the Middle East. Were the Nephites growing local American crop plants by this time? That seems a little odd, since there is no mention of Lehi’s family encountering native people in what is consistently described in the BoM as “wilderness.”

2 Nephi 5:13–15 says that Nephi’s people had swords and that Nephi taught them to work in iron, copper, brass, and steel. Later pre-Columbian Native Americans probably did not have iron, brass, or steel, except perhaps in very small amounts. Even in Jerusalem at the time of Lehi’s journey, steel would have been a very advanced technology that very few, if any, people would have had access to. Nephi appears in the BoM as a sort of multitalented superman, capable of building ships and cities, mining, making tools, hunting, prophesying, engraving records, building a nation, etc.

None of these anachronisms show definitely that the BoM is false. What they do suggest is that it is more likely than not that the BoM is a nineteenth-century forgery, not a historical record. About the best that can be said of the anachronisms is that they provide no support at all for the idea that the BoM was translated from a genuine ancient document.

Now, let’s take a look at the temple mentioned in 2 Nephi 5:16. This says: “And I, Nephi, did build a temple; and I did construct it after the manner of the temple of Solomon save it were not built of so many precious things; for they were not to be found upon the land, wherefore, it could not be built like unto Solomon’s temple. But the manner of the construction was like unto the temple of Solomon; and the workmanship thereof was exceedingly fine.” Keep in mind that according to the BoM itself, this was essentially a frontier society with its first generation still on the scene. Verse 28 indicates that at the time the temple was built, less than 30 years had passed since Lehi left Jerusalem. The whole idea of a significant number of fine craftspeople being available in such a frontier society is unlikely, much less their employment on a major building project like a temple. It’s not impossible, just unlikely. At best, this incident provides no support for the idea that the BoM is a true historical record.
