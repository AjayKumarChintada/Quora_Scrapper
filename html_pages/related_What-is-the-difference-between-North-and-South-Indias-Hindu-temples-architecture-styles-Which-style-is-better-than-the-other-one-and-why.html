There are three classifications for Hindu temple architecture: the Nagara or Indo-Aryan style in the north; the Dravida or southern style; and the Vesara or mixed style. Generally speaking, Hindu temples in North India are considerably smaller compared to South India. Many North Indian temples, or mandirs, are nestled in places with natural beauty, like mountaintops, river banks, caves, or gardens. In South India, the temple gateways are adorned with towering gopurams that lead to smaller interior towers; In the north, it’s the opposite. The shapes of the towers also vastly differ, with northern architecture peaking like a mountain and southern design built like a pyramid. Additionally, South Indian temples follow a fairly strict agamas culture that defines the worship, traditions, rituals, and protocols.

The architecture styles of Hindu temples in North and South India are distinct and have different characteristics.

North Indian temples, also known as Nagara style, typically have a tall and curvilinear shikhara (tower) over the sanctum, a mandapa (hall) in front of the sanctum, and sometimes smaller shrines in the complex. The shikhara is usually pyramid-shaped and may have elaborate carvings and sculptures.

South Indian temples, also known as Dravida style, are characterized by their pyramidal gopuram (tower) at the entrance, a large pillared hall, and a central shrine. The gopuram is usually square or rectangular in shape and may have many levels, each with elaborate carvings and sculptures.

It is not appropriate to say that one style is better than the other. Both styles are rich in history, art, and cultural significance and have evolved over centuries to suit the needs and traditions of their respective regions. The diversity in temple architecture is a testament to the richness of India's cultural heritage and serves as a visual representation of the diversity and unity of India's people.

Ultimately, the choice between North Indian and South Indian temple architecture styles is a matter of personal preference and aesthetic appreciation.
